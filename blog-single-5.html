How we came up with the puzzle games

By Marc • 3 mins read
Scroll

Why does the game have puzzle games?

Initially, the game's only interaction was between the character, with given answers. We quickly realized that this was insufficient to keep the player engaged and, especially, to establish a personal connection with the character Alex. To address this, we brainstormed elements that could be included while maintaining relevance to the story. Our first idea was a simple puzzle game where the player had to connect the right emotions with each other. Although emotions were a central theme in both our university project and the game itself, we concluded that this type of game wouldn't be enjoyable to play.

In search of an alternative, we considered a point-and-click game. These games are self-explanatory: players point and click on objects visible on their screens to interact with them. Given that point-and-click games should offer numerous clickable items, we chose to set a scene in Alex's room with his personal belongings, all of which have connections to the story. Since Alex studies art, the items in the room reflect his interests. Clicking on these belongings triggers new chat (answer) options for the player to engage in conversation with Alex about the related object.

After completing the initial game, we identified too many empty chapters without meaningful interaction besides talking to Alex. This led us to develop another game, revisiting the concept of our initial idea â€“ a puzzle game where connections are made. As emotions alone were deemed too mundane, we sought to tie the puzzle more closely to the story and Alex's character. The idea that emerged was a painting in many pieces that the player has to assemble to understand. Similar to the point-and-click game, new answer options should appear for the player, now related to the revealed picture.
